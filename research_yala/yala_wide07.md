# Facet: Demographic, Social & Household Data Sources

## City Municipality of Yala, Thailand - Comprehensive Research Report

**Date compiled:** 2025  
**Research scope:** All demographic, social, household, and population data sources at provincial, district, and subdistrict levels for Yala Municipality and Yala Province.

---

## Key Findings

- Yala Province had a registered population of approximately **552,479 persons** (2024) with **180,582 households** [^1^][^424^], making it one of the fastest-growing provinces in Thailand with a TFR of 1.98 [^422^].
- Yala is **79.6% Muslim** (as of 2014 data), part of the Malay-speaking Patani Muslim community, with the Jawi language widely spoken [^336^][^335^]. The province has approximately **3,900 mosques** nationally with a significant concentration in Yala [^337^].
- The **2020 Population and Housing Census was postponed due to COVID-19** and rescheduled for 2025 (data collection: April 1 - June 19, 2025) [^323^][^11^]. The 2010 Census microdata (1% sample) is available through ILO and NSO data catalogs [^324^][^325^].
- Yala's **Human Achievement Index (HAI) 2022 value of 0.6617** ranks 11th nationally ("high" category), with particularly strong employment ranking (6th) but weak education ranking (66th) [^428^].
- **Poverty rate**: Approximately **20.83% of Yala's population (208,274 persons)** were classified as poor in 2020, with poverty distributed across all 8 districts [^376^].
- **Aging society**: Yala entered an "Aged society" in 2023 with an aging index of 55.09; elderly population reached **76,338 persons (13.89%)** in 2024 [^377^].
- **Labor force**: In 2024, Yala had a labor force participation rate of 67.98%, unemployment rate of 0.43%, with **61.80% of employment in agriculture** [^424^].
- **State welfare registrants**: In 2022, Yala had **143,351 welfare card holders** and **248,144 Khon La Khrueng program participants** [^395^].
- **Cross-border migration**: Significant Thai-Malay cross-border movement occurs, with Thais crossing to Malaysia for work in restaurants, fishing, and rubber plantations [^67^][^338^].
- **Conflict impact**: Since 2004, over 7,000 deaths and 14,000 injuries have occurred in the southern border provinces, profoundly affecting demographic and social data quality and collection [^426^].

- TPMAP Dashboard, TPMAP Analytics, TPMAP Pivot, TPMAP Logbook features [^326^]

**Source:** Academic research, IOM reports, ethnic studies  
**URLs:** [Wikipedia - Patani Malays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patani_Malays) [^375^]; [Melbourne Asia Review](https://www.melbourneasiareview.edu.au/the-muslim-ethno-religiouscape-of-thailand-can-be-accommodated-peacefully/) [^335^]; [IOM Scoping Report](https://thailand.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1371/files/documents/2024-11/yala-scoping-report_2024.pdf) [^71^]

**Relevance:** ★★★★☆ Critical for understanding Yala's cultural context

**Key data:**
- **Patani Malays** (มลายูปัตตานี) are an ethnolinguistic Malay Muslim community native to Yala, Pattani, Narathiwat [^375^]
- Population: ~1.5 million (2018 est.) across all southern border provinces [^375^]
- Language: Kelantan-Pattani Malay (native), Thai, Southern Thai [^375^]
- Jawi script used for writing Malay [^71^]
- Historical ties to the Sultanate of Patani (annexed by Siam 1786, formalized by Anglo-Siamese Treaty 1909) [^375^]
- Close linguistic and cultural ties with Malays in Kelantan and Terengganu, Malaysia [^375^]
- Thailand does NOT collect official ethnicity data in census; all figures are estimates

## Migration Data

### 16. IOM Scoping Report - Southern Border Provinces (2024)

**Source:** International Organization for Migration (IOM) Thailand  
**URLs:** [IOM Yala Report](https://thailand.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1371/files/documents/2024-11/yala-scoping-report_2024.pdf) [^71^]; [IOM Overall Report](https://thailand.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1371/files/documents/2024-11/overall-scoping-report_2024.pdf) [^67^]; [IOM Narathiwat Report](https://thailand.iom.int/sites/g/files/tmzbdl1371/files/documents/2024-11/narathiwat-scoping-report_2024.pdf) [^8^]  
**Geographic granularity:** Village / Subdistrict / District / Provincial  
**Time series:** Single study (May-June 2024)  
**Access method:** Public reports (PDF)  
**Relevance:** ★★★★★ Most recent migration data for Yala

**Yala-specific findings [^71^]:**
- Non-Thai population in assessed areas: ~2,100 persons (~1% of assessed population)
- Myanmar nationals: ~1,400 (mainly Buddhist)
- Cambodian nationals: ~370 (mostly Muslim)
- Myanmar Muslims/Rohingya: ~130 (over 110 undocumented)
- Most non-Thais have lived in Thailand >10 years and are integrated
- Main occupations: construction, rubber plantations, fruit plantations, factories
- Drug abuse identified as major risk factor (affecting children as young as 11)

**Cross-border mobility [^67^]:**
- Thais cross to Malaysia for work in restaurants, fishing, rubber plantations
- Non-Thais transit through Yala to Malaysia
- Betong POE (formal Point of Entry) observed; no semi-formal or informal POEs identified in Yala

**Overall SBP findings [^67^]:**
- 40,000+ non-Thais identified across all 5 provinces
- 13,000+ undocumented migrants (mostly in Pattani)
- ~310 Myanmar Muslims/Rohingya across provinces

## Trends & Signals

### Demographic Trends
- **Rapid aging**: Yala's elderly population increased from 11.16% (2014) to 13.89% (2024), entering "Aged society" status in 2023 [^377^]. Betong district has the highest aging rate at 14%.
- **High fertility**: Yala's Total Fertility Rate of 1.98 is among the highest in Thailand (ranked 3rd after Pattani 2.00 and Narathiwat 1.85), contrasting with the national average of ~1.5 [^422^].
- **Agriculture dependency**: 61.80% of employment in agriculture/fishing (2024), significantly higher than national average [^424^].
- **Declining unemployment**: Unemployment dropped from 1.31% (2020) to 0.43% (2024), possibly reflecting underemployment rather than full employment [^424^].

### Social Trends
- **Poverty persistence**: Despite national poverty decline, Yala maintains a poverty rate of ~20-39% depending on measurement (income vs. MPI), among the highest in Thailand [^376^].
- **High informal employment**: 198,150 informal workers (75.7% of employed), concentrated in agriculture and self-employment [^424^].
- **Low education attainment**: 31.52% of workers have only primary education, with Yala ranking 66th in education component of HAI [^424^][^428^].
- **State welfare dependency**: 143,351 welfare card holders + 248,144 stimulus recipients indicate heavy reliance on government support [^395^].

### Migration Trends
- **Cross-border labor mobility**: Significant Thai-Malay worker movement for employment in fishing, restaurants, and plantations [^336^][^67^].
- **Conflict-driven outmigration**: Research indicates insurgency violence increases outmigration rates from southern border provinces [^338^].
- **Limited international migration to Yala**: Only ~2,100 non-Thais identified in assessed communities, mostly long-term residents integrated into local society [^71^].

### Welfare Trends
- **Aging welfare gap**: Only 98 elderly (0.14%) are in the social security system; the vast majority depend on old-age allowances [^377^].
- **Housing needs**: Significant housing assistance programs ongoing, with 234 houses for low-income persons and 545 for elderly under construction (2020 data) [^376^].

## Controversies & Limitations

### Data Quality Issues
1. **Census postponement**: The 2020 Population and Housing Census was postponed to 2025 due to COVID-19, leaving a 15-year gap between censuses [^11^][^323^]. This severely limits current demographic data accuracy.

2. **No ethnicity data**: Thailand does not collect official ethnicity data in its census. All Malay/Muslim population figures are estimates from various sources, creating inconsistency (ranging from 79.6% to 82% Muslim for Yala) [^336^][^335^].

3. **Undercounting of conflict-affected populations**: Security concerns in certain districts limit data collection access. IOM reports that "specific sub-districts and villages were avoided due to potential security threats" [^71^].

4. **Informal employment undercounting**: The Labor Force Survey likely undercounts informal and seasonal workers, particularly in agriculture and cross-border labor [^424^].

5. **Refugee/migrant undercounting**: Undocumented Myanmar Muslims/Rohingya and other irregular migrants are systematically undercounted. IOM estimates 110+ undocumented in Yala alone [^71^].

### Sensitivity Issues
6. **Conflict data sensitivity**: Migration and violence data in southern border provinces is politically sensitive. The IOM report is marked "FOR RESTRICTED DISTRIBUTION ONLY DUE TO SENSITIVE CONTENT" [^71^].

7. **Poverty measurement discrepancies**: Different poverty measures yield vastly different results for Yala:
   - Income poverty: ~6.53% (official rate, likely understated) [^376^]
   - MPI-based (TPMAP): ~20.83% [^376^]
   - Pre-2004 conflict era: 33% [^429^]

8. **Religious demographic sensitivity**: Precise mosque locations and Muslim population counts can be sensitive in the conflict context. Some sources report different figures.

9. **Cross-border movement**: Thai-Malay cross-border labor mobility is largely informal and difficult to track through official channels [^336^].

10. **Data collection bias**: Key informant interviews in conflict-affected areas suffer from "concerns over government reprisal" and "reluctance to share information" [^71^].

## Recommended Deep-Dive Areas

### 1. TPMAP Subdistrict-Level Poverty Data
**Why:** TPMAP provides the most granular poverty data available (down to village level in some areas) using the MPI framework. Accessing Yala's subdistrict-level TPMAP data would enable precise poverty targeting for municipal planning. Contact NESDB/NECTEC for API access [^96^].

### 2. 2010 Census Microdata for Yala
**Why:** The 1% microdata sample (538,463 cases, 83 variables) includes migration, housing, fertility, education, and employment modules. Extracting Yala cases would provide the richest demographic dataset available. Request via NSO Data Center [^324^].

### 3. NSO Labor Force Survey Quarterly Data for Yala
**Why:** Quarterly LFS data from 2021-2024 would reveal seasonal employment patterns, critical for an agricultural province like Yala. Contact Yala Provincial Statistical Office [^424^].

### 4. DOPA Registered Population by Subdistrict
**Why:** DOPA maintains the most current population data with monthly updates. Obtaining subdistrict-level data for all of Yala's tambons would provide the basis for accurate demographic planning. Access via DOPA statistics portal or provincial office.

### 5. CDD Basic Minimum Need Village Data
**Why:** CDD's annual BMN survey provides village-level data on housing, water, sanitation, health, education, and income dimensions. This is the primary data source feeding into TPMAP and would be invaluable for identifying specific community needs within Yala municipality.

### 6. Cross-Border Migration Patterns
**Why:** Given Yala's border location with Malaysia and significant cross-border labor mobility, understanding migration patterns is critical for labor market planning. IOM's scoping exercise provides a starting point [^71^], but more systematic flow monitoring data would be valuable.

### 7. Gender and Youth Disaggregation
**Why:** With female labor force participation of only 44.24% and high informal employment (75.7%), understanding gender-specific barriers and youth (17-25 years: 63,835 persons) employment transitions is critical for inclusive municipal planning [^424^][^376^].

### 8. Conflict Impact on Demographics
**Why:** The ongoing insurgency has caused 7,000+ deaths and 14,000+ injuries since 2004 [^426^]. Understanding how violence affects population distribution, outmigration, and service delivery is essential for Yala municipality.
